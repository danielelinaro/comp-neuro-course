Contents of the course

This course will provide students with an understanding of fundamental concepts of Computational Neuroscience, a multidisciplinary field studying how brains process and store information. The students will be introduced to the tools of dynamical systems theory employed throughout the course, and will learn the concept of neuron model and its relationship to neurophysiology. The course will follow a "reductionist" approach, from detailed single-compartment neuron models of the Hodgkin-Huxley family, to simplified integrate-and-fire models, and finally to rate models of interacting neuronal populations. The course will teach the student the most appropriate level of description for understanding a given neurophysiological phenomenon.

The contents of the course are detailed in the following. Please bear in mind that these might change to accommodate the interests of the students enrolled in the course.

Part 1: Mathematical background

  • Functions and their graphical representation.
  • Ordinary differential equations: theory and the forward Euler method of solution.
  • Dynamical systems theory: phase portraits and bifurcations.

Part 2: Neurophisiology

  • Cellular excitability and the action potential.
  • Lipid bilayer: the cell membrane as a capacitance.
  • Ionic currents and ion channels as time-varying conductances.
  • The Nernst equation: electrochemical equilibrium of a single ion species across the cell membrane.
  • The Goldman-Hodgkin-Katz equation: ionic flux of several ion species as a function of the membrane potential.

Part 3: The Hodgkin-Huxley neuron model

  • Definition of the model.
  • Ion channel dynamics during an action potential.
  • Fast inactivating sodium and delayed rectifier potassium currents: the basic building blocks of neuronal excitability.
  • A-type potassium currents and type-I and type-II excitability.

Part 4: The family of integrate-and-fire (IF) neuron models

  • IF models as a reduction of the HH model.
  • The leaky IF model: equation, reset, analytical derivation of the frequency-current (f-I) relationship.
  • Refractory period and consequences on the shape of the f-I curve.
  • Adaptive exponential IF model: properties and simple bifurcation analysis.

Part 5: Synaptic transmission

  • Chemical synapses: neurotransmitter types and post-synaptic receptors.
  • Kinetic model of synaptic transmission.
  • Short-term synaptic plasticity.

Part 6: Networks of neurons

  • Neuronal populations as ensembles of individual neuron models.
  • Simplified rate models of homogeneous neural populations.
  • Oscillations in networks of excitatory and inhibitory populations.
  • Deep spiking neural networks for artificial intelligence applications.
